6.2.1 Composition Indicators

6.2.2 Mammal Species Richness

Mammal species richness is an appropriate indicator of biodiversity in sub-tropical dry forests as it is sensitive to habitat structure, resource availability, and anthropogenic pressures. Mammals occupy diverse ecological niches, and their presence often reflects overall ecosystem integrity, which serves as an effective proxy for measuring impacts of program activities on the environment (Ceballos & Ehlrich, 2002). Additionally, mammals are relatively easy to monitor through camera traps and transect surveys, which are easily replicable for monitoring over time, even in remote areas. Species richness is a simple analysis that can be done by basic training if communities are to take over on biodiversity monitoring.

Mammals were primarily monitored using camera traps that were placed in each ejido. The following procedure was used in the nine ejidos. 10 Browing® Dark Ops APEX 20MP brand camera traps or similar equipment was placed, except for those ejidos in which there were logistics problems in the field and only 5 or 6 cameras were placed. Each camera trap was placed in a site with natural vegetation coverage and preferably at the intersection of 2 to 3 trails, they were attached to the base of the trunks at a height of 40 cm from the ground. The cameras were set to take a photo and a 15-second video, they remain active 24 hours a day for the duration of the study (2 weeks).

Each camera trap had a unique name and GPS coordinate to identify which camera captured which animal. Once we were able to get the raw data, we also needed to ensure that the data collected from the baseline assessment of the carbon project aligned with the Nature Framework requirements. We found that the data collected at each camera trap could have been associated with a different functional group, so we took each location of camera trap and added it to the shapefile of the functional groups. We then cross referenced this file to the raw data collected in the field according to the camera trap ID and coordinates. If the camera trap was in an area not described as Tropical/Sub-tropical dry forests, the data was excluded.

To calculate the species richness, we measured the total number of species observed in each ejido and added them together for the Project Extent, while excluding any species that was found outside of the tropical/sub-tropical dry forests. Avian Species Richness

Bird species richness is an appropriate indicator for sub-tropical dry forests due to birds’ ecological sensitivity, habitat specificity, and functional diversity. The dry forests in the Project Extent are characterized by seasonal rainfall, deciduous vegetation, and high biodiversity, which allows for a wide array of bird species. Many of these species are habitat specialists or migratory species that rely on highly intact structure and seasonal resources. Birds respond quickly to changes in vegetation cover, forest structure and water availability, making them reliable early indicators of ecosystem health. Program activities are aimed at improving forest structure through corridor development, conservation efforts, and increasing water availability in response to climate change. Changes in species richness over the program lifetime could be reliably attributed to changes due to program activities.

Avian species were monitored with point counts along transects during the two week long baseline biodiversity assessment in 2023. The following procedure was used to collect observations of avian species. The counting method was used by transects separated by 1 km between each transect. The criterion for choosing the transects was the proximity of the points where the camera traps were installed but separated at the beginning between 200 and 300 meters, so that the noise from the installation of the cameras would not affect the bird recording. The observation points were separated from each other every 100 m, at each point all the birds observed were recorded for 10 minutes.

At each point, the geographical coordinates, point number, time, species observed/heard, number of individuals per species, distance and observation height were taken. Another aspect to mention, in some points more transects were made and in others less, due to the conditions of the terrain and surface of the area. The recorded species were identified using binoculars (8x42) and the field guide “Go out and bird Yucatán” (2014) and the Merlin application (The Cornell Lab).

Each coordinate for point counts (observation points) was included in the raw data and were cross referenced with the location of each coordinate and the associated functional group. Any point and corresponding data was excluded if it was not located in the Tropical/Sub-tropical dry forests. [MM4] [MM5] 

To calculate the species richness, we measured the total number of species observed in each ejido and added them together for the Project Extent, while excluding any species that was found outside of the tropical/sub-tropical dry forests.

6.2.3 Herpetofauna Species Richness

Amphibians and reptiles are highly sensitive to changes in the environment. Reptiles (e.g., lizards, snakes, and turtles) are adapted to the warm, seasonally dry conditions characteristic of these forests. Many species are habitat specialists, occupying niches tied to particular vegetation types, ground cover, or thermal environments. Their population dynamics and community composition can change in response to alterations in land use, canopy cover, or fire regimes, making them effective indicators of habitat quality and connectivity.

Amphibians, while generally associated with moist habitats, are also important in dry forests — especially those that rely on ephemeral water sources for reproduction. Their permeable skin makes them vulnerable to changes in temperature, humidity, water availability, and pollutants. As a result, amphibians provide early warning signs of ecosystem degradation, particularly regarding hydrological balance and microhabitat integrity.

Monitoring the richness of both groups captures complementary aspects of ecosystem health: reptiles reflect structural and thermal habitat features, while amphibians respond to moisture dynamics and microhabitat quality. Additionally, these taxa are often underrepresented in conservation assessments, so their inclusion supports a more holistic and taxonomically inclusive biodiversity strategy. It is important to note that monitoring did not occur during the wet season, when herpetofauna richness and diversity would be at its highest. Unfortunately, due to the remoteness of most of the ejidos and the transects, it is nearly impossible to navigate to some of them in the wet season. While we would not expect to see the highest species richness each monitoring period, we do expect to see moderate richness and will provide a good indication of ecosystem health even in the dry season, when climate may be most impactful on the environment.

The following procedure was used to monitor the herpetofauna species in the Project Extent. Transects of 1000 meters in length and 5 meters in width was chosen, during which a thorough search was carried out in all the microhabitats present within the transect, where the presence of herpetofauna was considered probable (under stones, under logs, in tree cavities, between tree bark, in burrows in the ground, etc.).

The number of transects in each ejido varied depending on its area; we sought to cover the greatest heterogeneity of the landscape present in each locality. The lizards were captured using thick rubber bands, throwing them by stretching them with a finger and oriented towards the individual, so that when hitting it they produce a temporary immobilization that allows their capture without causing damage to the specimen. The study was complemented by reviewing water sources and wells in those locations where there were bodies of water. The amphibians and reptiles found were identified to species level using specialized field guides and were recorded in a field log, along with the number of the specimen, the ejido where it was found, the number of the transect and any characteristic or observation of importance for this monitoring.

To determine which transects needed to be excluded from the study, the transect coordinates were cross referenced to the IUCN functional groups. Because transects were followed for these observations, multiple points along the transect were assessed to determine what functional group they were located in. Each transect had an initial and final coordinate along the transect. [MM6] 

To calculate the species richness, we measured the total number of species observed in each ejido and added them together for the Project Extent, while excluding any species that was found outside of the tropical/sub-tropical dry forests.  

6.2.4 Structure Indicators

6.2.4.1 Aboveground biomass

Aboveground biomass is a widely used indicator for ecosystem structure, carbon storage, and habitat quality in subtropical dry forests. Aboveground biomass reflects the accumulation of woody materials (trees, shrubs, and lianas), which influences the ecological functions and services of the ecosystem. In dry forest ecosystems, changes in AGB are related to land-use practices such as deforestation, grazing, and fuelwood extraction. Thus, monitoring AGB helps detect degradation and recovery over time, providing insight into the effectiveness of program activities (i.e., Assisted Native Regeneration in wildlife corridors, improved forest management). AGB is quantifiable through standardized field measurements and remote sensing, making it a practical and scalable indicator for monitoring ecosystem condition across large areas and over time.

6.2.4.2 Canopy Cover

Canopy cover is a suitable indicator for subtropical dry forests because it reflects forest condition, habitat quality, and restoration progress. It influences microclimate, soil moisture, and biodiversity, particularly for forest-dependent species—and can be easily monitored using remote sensing or field tools. Its sensitivity to disturbance and recovery makes it a reliable measure of ecosystem health.

Evidence of the documented inventory used to establish reference values for Condition indicators is maintained through a structured compilation of peer-reviewed literature, spatial datasets, and reproducible analytical workflows. All data sources, selection criteria, and analytical steps were recorded and archived to ensure transparency, traceability, and auditability in accordance with Verra requirements.

For biodiversity composition indicators, including mammal, bird, and herpetofauna species richness, the documented inventory consists of peer-reviewed scientific studies published from 2000 onward and primarily conducted in the Calakmul Biosphere Reserve (CBR) and the broader Yucatan Peninsula. The CBR was selected as the primary reference area because it represents near-pristine ecological conditions where most anthropogenic activities are prohibited. Literature searches were conducted systematically using Google Scholar with predefined keyword combinations such as “species richness,” “diversity,” “Calakmul Biosphere Reserve,” and “Yucatan Peninsula,” tailored to each taxonomic group. Records of search terms, publication dates, and inclusion criteria were maintained as part of the inventory documentation.

For mammal species richness, the inventory includes camera-trap and field-based studies focusing on different mammalian groups within the CBR. These include studies on ungulates documenting six species (Reyna-Hurtado & Tanner, 2007) and broader assessments of large terrestrial mammals across the Yucatan Peninsula, identifying a total of 14 large mammal species (Reyna-Hurtado et al., 2015). Because many studies focused on specific taxa, results were synthesized across multiple sources to produce a comprehensive mammal species inventory (excluding bats), resulting in a reference value of 46 species. All source publications are cited and archived as part of the documented inventory.

For bird species richness, the inventory draws on multiple avifaunal surveys conducted within the CBR. These include a study assessing bird abundance across feeding guilds that identified more than 300 species (Walsh, 2018), as well as a comprehensive inventory documenting 403 bird species, which represents the most extensive avian dataset available for the reserve (González et al., 2016). These studies were used to inform a conservative reference value of 230 bird species, reflecting expected richness under intact ecological conditions while accounting for methodological variability among surveys.

For herpetofauna species richness, the documented inventory includes post-2000 studies conducted in the CBR using standardized field methods such as Visual Encounter Surveys and pitfall traps. A key study documented the presence of 20 amphibian species and 69 reptile species, resulting in a total of 90 herpetofauna species under near-pristine conditions (Colston et al., 2015). This study and supporting literature were archived as evidence of the reference condition.

For structural condition indicators where historical field-based reference data were limited, the documented inventory consists of spatially explicit remote sensing datasets and derived statistical analyses. Canopy cover reference values were based on Terra MODIS Vegetation Continuous Fields (VCF) products at 250 m resolution, generated annually using monthly composites of Terra MODIS land surface reflectance and temperature data across all seven spectral bands. Datasets covering the ten years preceding project start (t–10 to t=0) were included in the inventory, along with ecosystem classification layers and Habitat Conversion Risk maps used to filter pixels.

Aboveground biomass reference values were derived from a documented inventory of GEDI Level 4 Aboveground Biomass data combined with Sentinel-1 synthetic aperture radar and Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery. A Random Forest regression model was trained annually using approximately 4,000 GEDI plots per year to predict AGB across the program’s ecoregion. All input datasets, model parameters, training samples, and output rasters were stored with accompanying metadata describing spatial resolution, temporal coverage, and ecosystem filtering criteria.

All analytical procedures used to derive percentile-based reference values (75th percentile for canopy cover and 90th percentile for aboveground biomass) were fully documented, including pixel selection rules, statistical assumptions, and sample sizes. Reproducible scripts and summary outputs were archived to enable independent verification.[MM7]

6.8.1 Drivers of Biodiversity Loss

The parameters used for the drivers of biodiversity loss were measured in hectares of forest loss per year. The five main drivers identified were agricultural conversion, illegal logging, forest fires, cattle ranching, and hunting and poaching. The research that was used to identify these drivers and measure the parameters of each driver corresponds to the Yucatan Peninsula of Mexico and then made proportional through a simple calculation to the number of hectares of the Project Extent.

The literature review synthesized peer-reviewed studies and technical reports that examined patterns, drivers, and consequences of biodiversity loss within and around the Calakmul Biosphere Reserve and the broader Selva Maya region. This body of literature consistently identified land-use change, agricultural expansion, habitat fragmentation, wildfire, and climate variability as the primary pressures affecting forest structure, species composition, and ecosystem function (Mittermeier et al., 2003; Aalen et al., 2015; Réjou-Méchain et al., 2021).

Landscape-scale analyses using remote sensing and spatial modeling documented long-term forest cover change and increasing fragmentation driven by agricultural conversion and human settlement (Hansen et al., 2013; Ellis et al., 2017). These studies demonstrated that even small-scale productive activities had cumulative impacts on habitat connectivity and biodiversity at the regional level. Biodiversity monitoring and climate-focused research further highlighted the compounding effects of drought, altered precipitation patterns, and temperature variability, which exacerbated species vulnerability and reduced ecosystem resilience (IPBES, 2019; FAO, 2020).

At finer ecological scales, research on traditional land-use systems showed that biodiversity outcomes were highly context-dependent. Studies of Mayan agroforestry and ejido-based land management indicated that traditional practices maintained relatively high levels of plant and faunal diversity compared to intensive agricultural systems (Toledo et al., 2003; Porter-Bolland et al., 2012). Complementary studies on wetlands, soil microbial communities, and aquatic ecosystems emphasized the sensitivity of hydrological systems to land degradation and fire disturbance, revealing cascading effects across trophic levels and ecosystem services (Díaz et al., 2018; Allen et al., 2020). Collectively, this literature established a strong empirical foundation for understanding how interacting social, ecological, and climatic drivers contributed to biodiversity loss in the region.

6.8.2 Implementation of Program Activities

The implementation years for each long-term outcome of the program were then calculated using the long-term implementation plan for the 40-year program period and how effective each outcome or activity is at reducing biodiversity loss drivers.

The following literature review focused on the effectiveness of conservation and management interventions aimed at mitigating biodiversity loss and strengthening ecosystem resilience. These studies evaluated a range of strategies, including habitat connectivity planning, biodiversity and species monitoring, community-based patrolling, wildfire management, watershed protection, and alternative livelihood programs (Margules & Pressey, 2000; Gardner et al., 2013).

Research on ecological connectivity emphasized the importance of maintaining and restoring corridors to counteract fragmentation and sustain species movement across landscapes. Spatial planning frameworks and connectivity metrics were shown to support prioritization of conservation actions and improve long-term biodiversity outcomes (Saura et al., 2017; Correa Ayram et al., 2019). Studies assessing biodiversity monitoring and community-based patrolling demonstrated that integrating local participation with satellite-based deforestation alerts improved detection of illegal activities and contributed to reduced forest loss (Bray et al., 2008; Global Forest Watch, 2021).

Literature addressing alternative livelihoods and adaptive management underscored the importance of sustained biodiversity monitoring and community engagement. Long-term monitoring frameworks enabled the detection of biodiversity change over time and supported evidence-based management decisions (Nichols & Williams, 2006; Lindenmayer & Likens, 2018). Livelihood diversification initiatives were found to help address underlying socioeconomic drivers of deforestation and habitat degradation, thereby reinforcing conservation outcomes when combined with governance and enforcement measures (Wunder et al., 2018).
