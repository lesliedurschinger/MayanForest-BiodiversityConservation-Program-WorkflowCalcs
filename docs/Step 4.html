Mammal Species Richness

Each camera trap had a unique name and GPS coordinate to identify which camera captured which animal. Once the raw data was collected and reviewed, it was aligned with the Nature Framework requirements. The data collected at each camera trap could have been associated with a different functional group, so each location was taken and added to the shapefile of the functional group.

Then, this file was cross referenced to the raw data collected in the field according to the camera trap ID and coordinates. If the camera trap was in an area not described as Tropical/Sub-tropical dry forests, the data was excluded, as seen in Table 8.

Avian Species Richness

At each point, the geographical coordinates, point number, time, species observed/heard, number of individuals per species, distance and observation height were taken. Another aspect to mention, in some points more transects were made and in others less, due to the conditions of the terrain and surface of the area. The recorded species were identified using binoculars (8x42) and the field guide “Go out and bird Yucatán” (2014) and the Merlin application (The Cornell Lab).

Each coordinate for point counts (observation points) was included in the raw data and were cross referenced with the location of each coordinate and the associated functional group. Any point and corresponding data was excluded if it was not located in the Tropical/Sub-tropical dry forests, as seen in Table 9.

Aboveground Biomass

To ensure high-precision and replicable values of Aboveground Biomass (AGB), raw GEDI data was used: Spaceborne LiDAR: NASA GEDI Level 4A (GEDI04_A_002)

The Global Ecosystem Dynamics Investigation (GEDI) L4A Monthly dataset was utilized as the primary source for biomass calibration. GEDI provides high-resolution laser ranging observations of the Earth’s 3D structure from the International Space Station. Footprint-level estimates of Aboveground Biomass Density (AGBD) were extracted and used directly.

GEDI’s LiDAR technology provides direct measurements of canopy height and vertical profile, which are more strongly correlated with biomass than passive optical signals alone. This minimizes the risk of “signal saturation” in high-biomass areas.

Canopy Cover

The canopy cover raster was retrieved from the NASA hub and the product is described in Step H3. Canopy Cover directly measures habitat structure and is highly sensitive to both ecosystem degradation and conversion.

The indicator has 10 years of continuous historical data, enabling regression-based forecasting as required by SD VISta for using historical conversion rates MODIS is a credible, globally recognized source with documented accuracy and validation Spatial resolution (250m) complies with SD VISta requirement of ≤300m.

The indicator is already used for program monitoring, ensuring consistency between baseline and verification. Compliance with Section 7.5.4: Dataset meets all data requirements including spatial resolution, temporal coverage, credible source, and sensitivity to habitat conversion.
